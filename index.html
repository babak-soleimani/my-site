I work across music, spatial research, and cultural analysis. My practice is concerned with how interiors shape listening — how light, surfaces, and arrangement can expand or compress the sense of presence between bodies.

I perform with AgawDilim, a project that blends modal improvisation, microtonal drift, and rhythmic architectures influenced by jazz and regional classical traditions.

Parallel to performance, I study the design histories of cafes and social rooms. Using open data (Yelp), I map the shift from intimate, shadowed “nook” interiors to the contemporary “airspace” aesthetic — the global coffee-shop interior of bright walls, hanging plants, and laptop-compatible neutrality.

I am interested in how aesthetic forms circulate through global supply networks, how style encodes class aspiration, and how listening can be used as a way of reading architecture.
